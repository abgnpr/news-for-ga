### IT Industry AI Revenue to Cross $10–12 Billion in FY26 — Nasscom Annual Review

India's IT services industry AI revenue is estimated upwards of $10–12 billion for FY26, reflecting a shift from experimentation to scaled enterprise deployments with measurable ROI, per Nasscom's Annual Strategic Review 2026. Total IT industry revenue is projected at $315 billion in FY26, a 6.1% increase. About 70% of the top 25 IT providers have acquired AI-native capabilities through inorganic growth, and 85% have some form of agentic AI platform, with 35–40% offering domain-specific vertical agentic AI platforms. Industry headcount is expected to grow 2.3% in FY26, while over 20 lakh professionals were upskilled in AI, including 2–3 lakh in advanced AI skills. Nasscom noted that 2025 marked a decisive inflection point where AI moved from experimentation to function-specific, measurable ROI and outcome-led transformation.

**Perspective & Context:**
- *In simple terms:* India's IT companies are now earning $10–12 billion just from AI services — a segment that barely existed two years ago. The bigger shift is that AI is no longer a pilot project; companies are deploying it at scale and measuring actual returns. Most top IT firms have bought AI startups or built AI agent platforms to stay competitive.
- **Agentic AI platforms** — AI systems that can autonomously perform multi-step tasks (like processing invoices, resolving customer queries, or managing supply chains) rather than just answering questions; 85% of top 25 IT providers now offer these
- **AI revenue ($10–12 billion) in context:** This is roughly 3–4% of the industry's $315 billion total revenue — small but growing rapidly. For comparison, cloud services took nearly a decade to reach a similar share of IT revenue
- The 20 lakh professionals upskilled in AI represents a massive reskilling effort — roughly 1 in 3 of India's ~55 lakh IT workforce has undergone some form of AI training, though only 2–3 lakh have advanced skills
- The 6.1% overall industry growth is modest by historical standards (pre-pandemic growth was 8–10%), reflecting cautious client spending amid global uncertainty — but AI is emerging as the key growth driver going forward

### DRDO Advancing AI for Defence — Trustworthiness Frameworks, Autonomous Systems, and Sovereign Data Infrastructure

DRDO is actively deploying AI across defence applications including ISR (Intelligence, Surveillance and Reconnaissance), man-machine teaming, natural language processing, and unmanned autonomous systems, with trustworthiness evaluation and independent verification & validation frameworks released in 2025–2026. The Centre for Artificial Intelligence & Robotics (CAIR) leads fundamental and applied AI research covering machine perception, decision support, reasoning systems, and knowledge extraction for battlefield use. A dedicated DRDO Young Scientist Laboratory for AI (DYSL-AI) at Bengaluru focuses on advanced AI technologies and language translation for situational awareness. At the institutional level, the Defence AI Council (DAIC, established February 2019) provides strategic direction, while the Defence AI Project Agency (DAIPA) executes deployable AI projects. DRDO is also mandating data standardisation across battlefield, ISR, UAV, naval, and cyber domains through its ETAI Framework (October 2024), and emphasises sovereign data localisation with air-gapped networks for classified data. The government is planning a five-layer AI Stack Strategy covering application, model, compute, infrastructure, and energy layers.

**Perspective & Context:**
- *In simple terms:* India's defence research agency DRDO is integrating AI into military systems — from drones that can operate on their own to systems that help commanders make faster decisions on the battlefield. Crucially, they're also building rules to ensure this AI is trustworthy and safe, and insisting that sensitive military data stays on Indian servers, not on foreign cloud platforms.
- **DAIC (Defence AI Council)** — the top decision-making body for AI adoption in India's military, set up under the Ministry of Defence; it decides strategy and priorities. **DAIPA (Defence AI Project Agency)** is its executive arm that turns those strategies into actual deployed systems
- **What DRDO's ETAI Framework does:** Standardises how military data is labelled, versioned, and securely handled across all AI/ML systems — think of it as creating a common language so that data from a drone, a naval radar, and a cyber sensor can all be fused and analysed together
- **Manav for AI** — the PM's guiding principles for India's AI strategy, standing for Moral & ethical systems, Accountable governance, National sovereignty, Accessible & inclusive technology, and Valid/legitimate systems
- India is drawing lessons from both the U.S. model (unified AI office integrating data, analytics, and cyber) and China's military-civil fusion model (rapid transfer of civilian AI breakthroughs to defence), while charting its own path through structured DRDO-academia-industry collaboration
- The emphasis on air-gapped sovereign infrastructure reflects a hard lesson from global conflicts: in wartime, dependence on foreign cloud providers is a strategic vulnerability — data must be accessible even if undersea cables or satellite links are disrupted

### India's Push to Train Homegrown LLMs — Sarvam AI, IndiaAI Mission, and the MoE Architecture

Bengaluru-based startup Sarvam AI released two Large Language Models (LLMs) at the AI Impact Summit — a 35 billion parameter model and a 105 billion parameter model — both trained from scratch on Indian soil using GPUs from the IndiaAI Mission's common compute cluster. The government provided Sarvam access to 4,096 GPUs, with the subsidy estimated at nearly ₹100 crore against a cluster bill of materials of ₹246 crore. The IndiaAI Mission has commissioned over 36,000 GPUs in data centres run by Indian firms like Yotta, offering startups and researchers compute at nominal fees. Sarvam's models use a Mixture of Experts (MoE) architecture, activating only a fraction of total parameters during inference to reduce cost and compute needs. The models claim improvements over comparable LLMs in Indian languages, though they have not yet been published on platforms like Hugging Face for independent verification. IIT Bombay-incubated BharatGen also trained a 17 billion parameter multilingual model on the same cluster, and Gnani.ai launched a small text-to-speech model.

**Perspective & Context:**
- *In simple terms:* India is trying to build its own AI models that work well in Indian languages, rather than relying on foreign models like ChatGPT or Gemini that are primarily designed for English. The government is subsidising the expensive computing hardware needed for this, and Sarvam AI is the first major result of that effort.
- **LLM (Large Language Model)** — an AI system trained on massive amounts of text data that can generate human-like responses; ChatGPT and Gemini are powered by LLMs.
- **GPU (Graphics Processing Unit)** — specialised chips originally designed for graphics but now essential for AI training; training an LLM requires thousands of GPUs running for weeks or months, costing millions of dollars.
- **MoE (Mixture of Experts)** — an architecture where only a small subset of the model's parameters are activated for each query, rather than using all of them. Think of it like a hospital with specialist doctors — instead of every doctor examining every patient, only the relevant specialists are called in, saving time and resources.
- **What the IndiaAI Mission is doing:** The government has built a shared pool of 36,000+ GPUs that startups can rent cheaply — like a co-working space for AI training, removing the biggest cost barrier for Indian firms.
- **Why Indian languages are hard for AI:** Indian languages are poorly represented on the internet compared to English, Korean, or Japanese. Many AI models work around this by translating Indian-language queries to English, processing them, and translating back — a workaround that adds cost and reduces quality.
- The ₹100 crore subsidy to Sarvam is roughly equivalent to what a mid-sized Indian startup might raise in a Series A funding round — highlighting how capital-intensive LLM training is even with government support.
