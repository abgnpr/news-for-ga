### Waaree Energies Breaks Ground for 10 GW Solar Ingot and Wafer Facility in Nagpur

Waaree Energies Ltd announced the groundbreaking of what it described as India's largest integrated solar ingot and wafer manufacturing facility at Butibori, Nagpur. The facility will house 10 GW capacity each for solar ingots and wafers — the critical upstream components of the solar photovoltaic value chain — on 300 acres at an investment of ₹6,200 crore. Once operational, it is expected to create over 8,000 direct and indirect jobs and significantly reduce India's reliance on imported ingots and wafers. The event was attended by Maharashtra CM Devendra Fadnavis and Union Minister Nitin Gadkari. Waaree currently operates solar PV module capacity of about 22.3 GW globally and solar cell manufacturing capacity of up to 5.4 GW.

**Perspective & Context:**
- *In simple terms:* Solar panels are made from wafers, which are sliced from ingots (large blocks of purified silicon). India makes plenty of solar panels but imports most of the raw ingots and wafers — mainly from China. Waaree is building a massive factory to make these in India, which would close a critical gap in the domestic solar supply chain.
- **Ingots and wafers in the solar value chain:** The chain runs: polysilicon → ingots → wafers → cells → modules (panels). India has been strong at the module (panel) end but weak at the upstream ingot/wafer stage. This 10 GW facility targets that exact gap.
- At ₹6,200 crore, this is one of the largest single investments in India's solar manufacturing ecosystem. For context, 10 GW of wafer capacity could supply enough material for solar panels to power roughly 7–8 million Indian homes annually.
- Waaree's existing 22.3 GW module capacity makes it India's largest solar panel manufacturer. Adding 10 GW of in-house ingot/wafer production means it can vertically integrate and reduce dependence on Chinese imports, which currently supply over 80% of global wafer production.

### Tamil Nadu Offers ₹2/Unit Power Subsidy for Eateries Switching from LPG to Electric Stoves

The Tamil Nadu government announced a subsidy of ₹2 per unit of electricity for restaurants, hotels, and tea shops that switch to electric stoves from commercial LPG cylinders, in response to LPG shortages caused by the West Asia conflict. The subsidy will remain in effect as long as the Centre's commercial LPG usage restrictions are in place. Additional Chief Secretary J. Radhakrishnan said electricity consumption by hoteliers and restaurants has already risen, with demand increasing by about 50 MW above average in recent days. Separately, the state announced that 60,698 factories operating with State Pollution Control Board consent will no longer require additional certificates to use alternative fuels such as kerosene. A State-level committee under the Chief Secretary has been formed to monitor systematic allocation of LPG cylinders to commercial establishments, with the Centre prioritising supply to hospitals and educational institutions.

**Perspective & Context:**
- *In simple terms:* The Gulf war has disrupted LPG shipments to India, creating shortages of commercial cooking gas. Tamil Nadu is responding by paying restaurants ₹2 per unit of electricity if they switch to electric stoves — essentially subsidising the transition away from gas dependence during the crisis.
- The 50 MW demand increase from eateries switching to electric cooking is roughly equivalent to the electricity consumption of a small town — showing how quickly commercial kitchens can shift the load from gas to the power grid when incentivised.
- This is a crisis-driven policy but could accelerate a longer-term shift: electric cooking is more energy-efficient (80–90% vs 40–50% for gas) and produces zero direct emissions. If eateries find electric stoves viable during this period, some may not switch back.
- The waiver of Pollution Control Board certificates for alternative fuels signals the urgency — the state is cutting red tape to let factories quickly switch to kerosene or other substitutes rather than wait for LPG supplies to normalise.
