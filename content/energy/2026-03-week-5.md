### CERC Amends Tariff Regulations to Integrate Energy Storage Systems as Core Grid Assets

On March 20, the Central Electricity Regulatory Commission (CERC) notified amendments to its tariff regulations, formally integrating energy storage systems (ESSs) into India's central tariff framework for the first time — treating them as core assets rather than pilot add-ons. The amendments extend the 2024 tariff framework to coal-, lignite-, and gas-based generating stations, as well as inter-State transmission systems (ISTS), when they deploy storage for beneficiaries. A standardised technical vocabulary is introduced covering battery cycle, depth of discharge, C-rate, state of charge, and round-trip efficiency. The framework establishes a supplementary tariff structure with separate fixed storage charges (recovering capital costs) and energy charges (reflecting the cost of charging electricity, adjusted for a minimum 85% round-trip efficiency and auxiliary consumption). Lithium-ion ESS assets are assigned a useful life of 15 years. Developers must file supplementary tariffs within 90 days of commissioning.

**Perspective & Context:**
- *In simple terms:* Until now, energy storage sat outside India's formal power tariff system — there was no clear rule on how storage operators would be paid. CERC's amendment fixes that: storage is now treated like a power plant, with defined charges and a clear payment structure. This gives investors and lenders the certainty they need to finance large-scale battery and pumped storage projects, which are essential for integrating the large volumes of solar and wind power India is adding.
- **CERC (Central Electricity Regulatory Commission)** — The central statutory body that regulates tariffs and conditions for inter-State power generation, transmission, and trading. State-level equivalents are SERCs (State Electricity Regulatory Commissions).
- **Fixed Storage Charge vs. Energy Charge** — Two-part tariff structure: the fixed charge recovers capital and fixed operating costs regardless of how much the storage is used (similar to capacity charges for power plants); the energy charge covers the cost of electricity used to charge the battery, adjusted for losses (round-trip efficiency must be at least 85%).
- **Round-trip efficiency** — The ratio of energy out to energy in for a storage cycle. An 85% minimum means for every 100 units of electricity used to charge, at least 85 units must be deliverable. This is the efficiency floor CERC has set for regulatory recognition.
- **ISTS (Inter-State Transmission System)** — The high-voltage transmission network connecting states, managed by PGCIL (Power Grid Corporation of India). Including ISTS in the ESS tariff framework means storage assets integrated with the national grid backbone are now formally regulated.
- The 15-year useful life for lithium-ion ESS and the 90-day filing window are specific regulatory parameters — both are standard MCQ-style facts for competitive exams covering energy regulation.

### CEA's 2026–35 Generation Adequacy Plan: 1,121 GW Installed Capacity, Coal Still 51% of Generation

The Central Electricity Authority (CEA) released its National Generation Adequacy Plan for 2026-27 to 2035-36, based on Monte Carlo simulations across thousands of demand-supply scenarios. Electricity demand is projected to rise from ~2,000 BU in 2025-26 to 3,365 BU by 2035-36; installed generation capacity must grow from ~520 GW to 1,121 GW, comprising 335 GW of fossil fuel-based capacity and 786 GW of non-fossil capacity. Despite coal's installed share falling from 41.5% to 28%, it will still account for 51% of actual electricity generation in 2035-36. Solar capacity will nearly triple to 509 GW but contribute only 27% of generation. Energy storage capacity is projected at 174 GW/888 GWh — dominated by pumped storage projects (PSPs), which surge from 5 GW to 94 GW, outpacing battery energy storage systems (BESS) growing from 9 GW to 80 GW. The CEA estimates India will need a Planning Reserve Margin (PRM) of 13–14% by 2035-36 to maintain grid resilience under high renewable penetration.

**Perspective & Context:**
- *In simple terms:* The CEA has mapped out what India's power system needs to look like a decade from now. The headline surprise: even though India is adding huge amounts of solar and renewables, coal will still generate more than half of India's electricity in 2035. Why? Because solar only shines during the day — coal fills the gaps. The plan also bets heavily on pumped storage (essentially giant water-based batteries) as the preferred solution for storing excess renewable power for later use.
- **Planning Reserve Margin (PRM)** — The extra generation capacity held above and beyond peak demand, expressed as a percentage. A 13–14% PRM means India needs ~13-14% more capacity than its highest expected demand, as a buffer against plant outages, low renewable output, or sudden demand spikes. The CEA sets this as the reliability threshold for a grid with high renewable penetration.
- **Pumped Storage Projects (PSPs)** — Electricity storage using two water reservoirs at different elevations: excess power pumps water uphill; when power is needed, water flows downhill through turbines. PSPs provide long-duration storage (typically 6+ hours), unlike most battery systems. The CEA prefers PSPs partly because BESS cells depend heavily on imports from "one Asian country" (China), creating supply chain and geopolitical risk.
- **BESS (Battery Energy Storage Systems)** — Grid-scale lithium-ion battery installations for storing renewable power. Despite rapid cost declines, the CEA flags risks: dependence on imported cells, depth-of-discharge limitations, degradation over cycles, and the need for "free charging power" — making actual costs harder to compare with PSPs.
- **Capacity vs. generation share gap** — Solar at 509 GW (46% of installed capacity) contributing only 27% of generation illustrates the "capacity utilisation" problem of renewables: solar panels sit idle at night and in monsoon clouds. Coal at ~28% capacity share delivers 51% of generation because it runs around the clock. This gap is the core reason storage and baseload capacity remain essential even in a renewable-heavy grid.
- Under-construction pipeline gives the plan credibility: 41 GW of coal plants already under construction, 155 GW of renewables under construction or tendering, 13 GW of pumped hydro under construction, and 6.6 GW of nuclear under construction — giving the CEA "complete visibility" on meeting targets.
