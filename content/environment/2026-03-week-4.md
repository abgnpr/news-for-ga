### Single-Use Plastic Ban Fails Ground Test: 84% of Surveyed Sites Still Using Banned Items

A field study by Toxics Link, a New Delhi-based environmental research and advocacy organisation, found that 84% of 560 locations surveyed across four cities — Bhubaneswar, Delhi, Guwahati, and Mumbai — continue using single-use plastic items banned nationwide since 2022. The survey, conducted between April and August 2025, covered a wide range of establishments including street vendors, juice stalls, markets, small restaurants, grocery stores, religious sites, railway platforms, and organised retail spaces. Bhubaneswar recorded the highest non-compliance at 89% of surveyed locations, followed by Delhi (86%), Mumbai (85%), and Guwahati (76%). The study found that thin plastic carry bags, disposable cutlery, cups, plates, and straws remained widespread in informal markets, while organised malls showed better adherence. About 91% of vendors said customers asked for carry bags, and 55% of customers brought their own bags. Vendors cited high customer demand and higher cost of alternatives as key reasons for continued plastic use. The report called for stronger national enforcement, regular inspections, coordinated regulatory action, and consistent penalties.

**Perspective & Context:**
- *In simple terms:* India banned single-use plastics three years ago, but the ban exists largely on paper — 84 out of every 100 locations surveyed still sell or use the banned items. The problem is not just awareness but enforcement: inspections are rare, penalties are inconsistent, and cheap plastic alternatives remain widely available.
- **Single-use plastic ban in India:** The government banned specific single-use plastic items from July 1, 2022, under the Plastic Waste Management Rules. Banned items include plastic earbuds, balloons sticks, flags, candy sticks, ice cream sticks, polystyrene (thermocol), plastic plates, cups, glasses, cutlery, straw, trays, wrapping or packing films, invitation cards, cigarette packets, and plastic or PVC banners below 100 microns.
- **Toxics Link** is a Delhi-based non-profit focused on environmental health and hazardous waste. Its reports often feed into policy discussions and international negotiations on waste management.
- The survey highlights a structural challenge: informal vendors, who make up the bulk of non-compliant sites, operate on thin margins and cannot easily absorb the cost premium of alternatives like paper cups or bagasse plates, which can cost 2–5 times more than plastic equivalents.
- India generates over 3.5 million tonnes of plastic waste annually — among the highest in the world. Single-use items are a disproportionate contributor because they are discarded immediately after use and difficult to collect in the informal waste stream.

### Chennai's Beach Sediments Contain Microplastics with Ecological Risks Beyond Their Low Abundance

A study by researchers at V.O. Chidambaram College, Thoothukudi, examined microplastic contamination in beach sediments from 15 sites along the Chennai coast. The findings show that while microplastic abundance is relatively low compared to many global beaches, the ecological risk remains significant because of the dominant polymer type — nylon fibres, which are more harmful than many other plastics. Most particles found were smaller than 1,000 μm, with fibres being the predominant shape. The study highlights a key disconnect: traditional monitoring focuses on microplastic counts, but polymer type, shape, and aging characteristics are equally or more important in determining ecological risk. Sources include damaged fishing nets and ropes, synthetic clothing (which sheds fibres during washing), tourism, and urban sewage and stormwater drains. Small organisms in beach sand — worms, crabs, shellfish — ingest these fibres, which block digestive tracts, and toxic compounds in the plastics can poison their bodies. Microplastics re-enter humans via contaminated seafood, carrying harmful chemicals and pathogens that cause inflammation and affect hormonal and immune systems over time.

**Perspective & Context:**
- *In simple terms:* Chennai's beaches look relatively clean on microplastic counts, but the type of plastic matters more than the quantity. The dominant contaminant — nylon fibres from fishing gear and synthetic clothes — is particularly harmful to marine life. These tiny particles enter the food chain through sand-dwelling organisms and eventually reach humans through seafood.
- **Microplastics** — plastic fragments smaller than 5 mm. They come from larger plastics breaking down (secondary microplastics) or are manufactured at that size (primary microplastics, like microbeads in cosmetics). Their small size makes them easy for organisms to ingest and nearly impossible to remove from the environment.
- **Why nylon is worse than other plastics:** Nylon is more chemically reactive and adsorbs pollutants from surrounding water more readily than polymers like polyethylene. Its fibre shape also makes it easier for organisms to ingest and harder to excrete, increasing internal damage.
- **The fishing gear problem:** The UN estimates that ~640,000 tonnes of fishing gear are lost or abandoned in oceans annually. In coastal cities like Chennai with active fishing industries, degrading nets and ropes are a major local source of microplastic fibres.
- A single load of synthetic laundry can release over 700,000 microplastic fibres into wastewater — most household washing machines and treatment plants are not equipped to filter them out.
