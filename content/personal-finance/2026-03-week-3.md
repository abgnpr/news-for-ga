### Index Outlook: War Wrecks Benchmarks — Nifty, Sensex, Bank Nifty All Break Key Supports

Nifty 50 (23,151), Sensex (74,564), and Nifty Bank (53,758) all tumbled over 5–7% last week as the Gulf conflict hammered Indian markets. FPIs sold a net $3.44 billion in the week and $5.7 billion in just the first two weeks of March. BSE Auto was the worst-hit sector, down ~10%. Key technical levels: Nifty's next support zone is 22,800–22,600, with 22,200–22,000 as the crucial floor — a break below 22,000 would signal serious trouble. Nifty Bank's support is at 53,300–53,500, with an extended fall target of 52,200. Sensex has supports at 72,700–72,250. The Smallcap 250 index broke below 15,000, opening the door to 14,000. On the upside, Nifty needs to reclaim 24,000 and Sensex 77,500 to restore bullish sentiment. The medium-to-long-term view remains intact as long as supports hold — Nifty Bank targets 64,000–65,000, Sensex targets 85,000–86,000, and Smallcap 250 retains its long-term target of 22,500–23,000 if 14,000 holds.

**Key Takeaways for Portfolio Decisions:**
- **FPI selling is the dominant short-term driver.** $5.7 billion out in two weeks is relentless. Until FPI flows stabilise, expect continued pressure — don't try to catch the falling knife.
- **Watch the floors, not the falls.** Nifty 22,000, Bank Nifty 52,200, Smallcap 250 at 14,000 — these are the make-or-break levels. A bounce from here keeps the long-term bullish case alive; a break below changes the game entirely.
- **Midcaps and smallcaps are in the danger zone.** Midcap 150 is sitting right on the 20,000 support, and Smallcap 250 has already broken 15,000. These segments face outsized downside risk if the sell-off deepens.
- **Any bounce near 23,700–24,000 (Nifty) or 55,500–56,000 (Bank Nifty) is likely a corrective pullback, not a reversal.** The technical view advises against aggressive buying until a sustained breakout above these resistance levels.
- **Long-term targets remain unchanged — but delayed.** The broader sideways range is intact. Patient investors with a 1–2 year horizon can accumulate selectively near support zones, but position sizing should reflect the elevated uncertainty.

### How Gold Has Stocks for Breakfast — A Decade of Outperformance

Over the one year ending March 13, 2026, gold (MCX spot) returned 82.86%, and only 37 out of ~1,134 NSE-listed stocks (3%) managed to beat it. The outperformance extends across all time horizons: over three years, gold returned 178.42% (only 13% of stocks beat it); over five years, 257.68% (21% beat it); over seven years, 390.34% (18% beat it); and over ten years, 443.37% (26% beat it). Gold's dominance isn't limited to crisis years — even in 2019, when gold returned a modest 6.57%, 84% of stocks still couldn't beat it. Valuation data reinforces the pattern: when Nifty 50's starting P/E is in the 20–25 band, gold outperforms the index by an average 11.27 percentage points over three years. Gold's average three-year returns across all starting valuation bands range from 46–62%. The only years equities dominated on breadth were 2021 (94% beat gold's 5.54%) and 2024 (75% beat gold's 14.85%).

**Key Takeaways for Portfolio Decisions:**
- **Gold is not just a crisis hedge — it's a structural performer.** The data shows gold beats most stocks even in non-crisis years. The "gold is dead money" narrative doesn't survive contact with decade-long data.
- **Equity valuations matter for gold allocation.** When markets are expensive (P/E 20–25), gold has historically outperformed Nifty by over 11 percentage points over three years. If current Nifty valuations are elevated, this argues for meaningful gold exposure.
- **97% of stocks failed to beat gold over one year.** This isn't about gold being exceptional — it's about the stock universe being ordinary. Most stocks don't deliver the heroic returns equity culture promises, especially in volatile markets.
- **Practical implication: maintain 10–15% gold in your portfolio** as a permanent allocation, not a tactical trade. Gold performs best precisely when you need it most — during expensive markets, geopolitical uncertainty, and liquidity crunches. SGB (Sovereign Gold Bonds), gold ETFs, and multi-asset allocation funds are efficient ways to hold it.
