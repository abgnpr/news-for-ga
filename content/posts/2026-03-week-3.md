### FPIs Sell ₹39,451 Crore from Indian Markets in One Week

`High` | Capital Markets Regulation

Foreign Portfolio Investors (FPIs) pulled out a net ₹39,450.60 crore from Indian markets over five consecutive sessions ending March 13, 2026, with equities bearing the brunt — gross equity sales totalled ₹23,457.02 crore against purchases of ₹16,081.97 crore. Total FPI selling through exchanges in March (till March 13) stood at ₹54,455 crore. Debt markets showed a mixed picture — 'Debt-VRR' recorded ₹87.89 crore inflow, but 'Debt-FAR' continued under pressure with ₹511.52 crore in outflows. On the derivatives side, FPI open interest in index futures rose to 2,87,383 contracts (₹44,829.76 crore) on March 13.

**Perspective & Context:**
- *In simple terms:* Foreign investors have been pulling money out of Indian stocks every single day in March — over ₹39,000 crore in just one week. This is driven by the Gulf war making global investors nervous, so they're moving money out of emerging markets like India and into safer assets like US bonds and gold.
- **FPI (Foreign Portfolio Investor)** — foreign institutions (hedge funds, pension funds, sovereign wealth funds) that invest in Indian stocks and bonds. Their buying or selling significantly moves Indian markets because they hold a large share of free-float market capitalisation.
- **VRR and FAR** — Voluntary Retention Route and Fully Accessible Route are two channels through which FPIs can invest in Indian government bonds. VRR requires investors to retain investments for a minimum period, while FAR bonds have no such restriction. The divergence (VRR inflows vs FAR outflows) suggests long-term committed investors are staying, while flexible money is leaving.
- At ₹54,455 crore of net selling in just 13 days of March, this is one of the most intense FPI sell-off months in recent years — comparable to the March 2020 COVID crash when FPIs sold over ₹60,000 crore in the full month.
- Meanwhile, SEBI Chairman Tuhin Kanta Pandey noted that SEBI has expanded the Electronic Book Provider (EBP) mechanism to include REITs and InvITs, and reduced the settlement cycle — measures aimed at improving market resilience during volatility.

### RBI Issues Draft Framework to Limit Customer Liability in E-Banking Fraud

`High` | Banking Sector

The RBI on March 6 issued draft directions revising its 2017 framework on limiting customer liability in unauthorised electronic banking transactions. Key provisions: a bona fide individual fraud victim can claim compensation of up to 85% of net loss or ₹25,000 (whichever is lower) for fraudulent transactions involving gross losses up to ₹50,000 — claimable only once in a lifetime. Customers get **zero liability** in two scenarios: (1) if the fraud occurred due to bank negligence/deficiency, or (2) if a third-party breach is reported within five calendar days. The cost-sharing formula for small-value compensation allocates 65% to the RBI, 10% to the customer's bank, and 10% to the beneficiary bank — initially proposed for one year, with the RBI's share to be reduced over time. The draft expands coverage from purely "unauthorised" transactions to "fraudulent electronic banking transactions," now including authorised transactions tainted by fraud (e.g., customer tricked into sending money to a scammer, transactions under coercion, or credentials obtained through phishing). Banks must provide 24x7 multi-channel reporting (phone, SMS, email, IVR, toll-free helpline), resolve complaints within 30 calendar days, and reverse fraudulent transactions with value dating from the original transaction date.

**Perspective & Context:**
- *In simple terms:* If someone hacks your bank account or tricks you into transferring money, the RBI wants clearer rules on who bears the loss. Under this new draft, if you report the fraud within 5 days and it wasn't your fault, the bank must reverse the full amount. For small frauds (up to ₹50,000), even if you're late reporting, you can still get up to ₹25,000 back — with the RBI itself funding most of the compensation.
- **What's changing from the 2017 rules:** The old framework only covered "unauthorised" transactions. The new draft also covers cases where you technically authorised a transaction but were deceived — like sending money to a scammer posing as a bank official, or approving a transaction under duress. This closes a major loophole that banks previously used to deny claims.
- **Zero liability with value dating** means if ₹20,000 was fraudulently debited on March 1 and reversed on March 20, the bank must calculate interest as though the money was never debited — the customer loses nothing, not even interest.
- The RBI funding 65% of compensation is unusual and signals how seriously it views digital fraud protection. This arrangement is explicitly temporary (one year), with the intent to gradually shift the burden entirely to banks — incentivising them to invest in better fraud prevention systems.

### India Engages with Iran, GCC, US to Secure LPG and LNG Transit Through Strait of Hormuz

`High` | Trade & Commerce

India has intensified diplomatic engagement with Iran, GCC countries, the US, and Israel to secure safe passage for energy carriers stranded near the Strait of Hormuz (SoH). Two Indian-flagged LPG carriers crossed the SoH on March 15 after talks led by PM Modi and External Affairs Minister S. Jaishankar. Multiple LPG, LNG, and crude oil tankers remain in the western approaches of the SoH awaiting safe transit, carrying a cumulative estimated 2.76 lakh tonnes of LPG — roughly a month's supply for India, whose average monthly LPG consumption stood at 2.81 million tonnes during April–February FY26.

**Perspective & Context:**
- *In simple terms:* The war near Iran has made the Strait of Hormuz — a narrow 34-km sea passage through which much of the world's oil and gas flows — dangerous for ships. India depends heavily on LPG imports through this route, so the government is using high-level diplomacy to get its ships through safely. Two ships made it; several more are still waiting.
- **Strait of Hormuz (SoH)** — a narrow waterway between Iran and Oman connecting the Persian Gulf to the Arabian Sea. About 20% of the world's oil passes through it, making it the most critical energy chokepoint globally.
- **What India is doing:** Engaging at the highest diplomatic levels — PM and EAM — with all key stakeholders (Iran, GCC, US, Israel) to ensure uninterrupted energy supply. Some ships are being rerouted through Khor Fakkan Port on Sharjah's east coast, which operates outside the Strait.
- The ~2.76 lakh tonnes of LPG stuck at sea is nearly one full month of India's consumption — meaning any prolonged disruption could directly affect cooking gas availability for crores of Indian households.
- The disruption is also hitting Indian agricultural exports to the Gulf — over 150 tonnes of perishable exports from Kerala are stranded, with air freight costs tripling from ₹70–80/kg to around ₹210/kg, making perishable exports commercially unviable.
- Globally, the US initiated a 400-million-barrel coordinated release from strategic petroleum reserves (including 86 million barrels from its own SPR) to stabilise oil prices amid the Hormuz disruption.

### Commerce Minister Goyal Promises More Support for Exporters Amid Gulf Conflict Disruptions

`High` | Trade & Commerce

Union Commerce Minister Piyush Goyal announced that the government is preparing a concrete support agenda for exporters amid trade disruptions caused by the Gulf conflict. The Commerce Ministry has devised insurance cover schemes to protect export cargo that may be damaged, lost, or delayed due to Red Sea or Strait of Hormuz shipping disruptions. The government has set up 24-hour helplines for exporters and importers, established SOPs in coordination with export promotion councils, and is conducting inter-ministerial consultations.

**Perspective & Context:**
- *In simple terms:* The Gulf war is disrupting shipping routes that Indian exporters depend on, causing delays, damaged cargo, and rising costs. The Commerce Minister is saying the government won't leave exporters to fend for themselves — insurance schemes, helplines, and a formal support package are coming.
- **What the government is doing:** Creating an insurance safety net so that if an exporter's cargo is damaged or delayed because ships can't pass through the Strait of Hormuz or Red Sea, the financial loss doesn't fall entirely on the exporter.
- **Export Promotion Councils** — industry bodies for specific sectors (textiles, engineering goods, spices, etc.) that work with the Commerce Ministry to promote and facilitate exports. They are now serving as the coordination layer between the government and affected exporters.
