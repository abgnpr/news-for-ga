### FPIs Sell ₹39,451 Crore from Indian Markets in One Week

`High` | Capital Markets Regulation

Foreign Portfolio Investors (FPIs) pulled out a net ₹39,450.60 crore from Indian markets over five consecutive sessions ending March 13, 2026, with equities bearing the brunt. Gross equity sales totalled ₹23,457.02 crore against purchases of ₹16,081.97 crore across the week. Daily net outflows ranged from ₹7,491.51 crore (March 13) to ₹8,405.83 crore (March 12, the heaviest single-day outflow). Total FPI selling through exchanges in March (till March 13) stood at ₹54,455 crore. Debt markets showed a mixed picture — 'Debt-General Limit' saw a net inflow of ₹503.92 crore and 'Debt-VRR' recorded ₹87.89 crore inflow, but 'Debt-FAR' continued under pressure with ₹511.52 crore in outflows. On the derivatives side, FPI open interest in index futures rose to 2,87,383 contracts (₹44,829.76 crore) on March 13. Market analysts attributed the relentless selling to continued stress from the West Asia conflict and the absence of diplomatic off-ramps.

**Perspective & Context:**
- *In simple terms:* Foreign investors have been pulling money out of Indian stocks every single day in March — over ₹39,000 crore in just one week. This is driven by the Gulf war making global investors nervous, so they're moving money out of emerging markets like India and into safer assets like US bonds and gold.
- **FPI (Foreign Portfolio Investor)** — foreign institutions (hedge funds, pension funds, sovereign wealth funds) that invest in Indian stocks and bonds. Their buying or selling significantly moves Indian markets because they hold a large share of free-float market capitalisation.
- **VRR and FAR** — Voluntary Retention Route and Fully Accessible Route are two channels through which FPIs can invest in Indian government bonds. VRR requires investors to retain investments for a minimum period, while FAR bonds have no such restriction. The divergence (VRR inflows vs FAR outflows) suggests long-term committed investors are staying, while flexible money is leaving.
- At ₹54,455 crore of net selling in just 13 days of March, this is one of the most intense FPI sell-off months in recent years — comparable to the March 2020 COVID crash when FPIs sold over ₹60,000 crore in the full month.

### RBI Issues Draft Framework to Limit Customer Liability in E-Banking Fraud

`High` | Banking Sector

The RBI on March 6 issued draft directions revising its 2017 framework on limiting customer liability in unauthorised electronic banking transactions. Key provisions: a bona fide individual fraud victim can claim compensation of up to 85% of net loss or ₹25,000 (whichever is lower) for fraudulent transactions involving gross losses up to ₹50,000 — claimable only once in a lifetime. Customers get **zero liability** in two scenarios: (1) if the fraud occurred due to bank negligence/deficiency, or (2) if a third-party breach is reported within five calendar days. The cost-sharing formula for small-value compensation allocates 65% to the RBI, 10% to the customer's bank, and 10% to the beneficiary bank — initially proposed for one year, with the RBI's share to be reduced over time. The draft expands coverage from purely "unauthorised" transactions to "fraudulent electronic banking transactions," now including authorised transactions tainted by fraud (e.g., customer tricked into sending money to a scammer, transactions under coercion, or credentials obtained through phishing). Banks must provide 24x7 multi-channel reporting (phone, SMS, email, IVR, toll-free helpline), resolve complaints within 30 calendar days, and reverse fraudulent transactions with value dating from the original transaction date.

**Perspective & Context:**
- *In simple terms:* If someone hacks your bank account or tricks you into transferring money, the RBI wants clearer rules on who bears the loss. Under this new draft, if you report the fraud within 5 days and it wasn't your fault, the bank must reverse the full amount. For small frauds (up to ₹50,000), even if you're late reporting, you can still get up to ₹25,000 back — with the RBI itself funding most of the compensation.
- **What's changing from the 2017 rules:** The old framework only covered "unauthorised" transactions. The new draft also covers cases where you technically authorised a transaction but were deceived — like sending money to a scammer posing as a bank official, or approving a transaction under duress. This closes a major loophole that banks previously used to deny claims.
- **Zero liability with value dating** means if ₹20,000 was fraudulently debited on March 1 and reversed on March 20, the bank must calculate interest as though the money was never debited — the customer loses nothing, not even interest.
- The RBI funding 65% of compensation is unusual and signals how seriously it views digital fraud protection. This arrangement is explicitly temporary (one year), with the intent to gradually shift the burden entirely to banks — incentivising them to invest in better fraud prevention systems.

### India Engages with Iran, GCC, US to Secure LPG and LNG Transit Through Strait of Hormuz

`High` | Trade & Commerce

India has intensified diplomatic engagement with Iran, GCC countries, the US, and Israel to secure safe passage for energy carriers stranded near the Strait of Hormuz (SoH). Two Indian-flagged LPG carriers — Shivalik and Nanda Devi — carrying approximately 46,000 tonnes each crossed the SoH on March 15 after talks led by PM Modi and External Affairs Minister S. Jaishankar. Six more LPG tankers and one LNG tanker remain in the western approaches of the SoH awaiting safe transit, carrying a cumulative estimated 2.76 lakh tonnes of LPG — roughly a month's supply for India, whose average monthly LPG consumption stood at 2.81 million tonnes during April–February FY26. Four crude oil tankers, three container ships, and two bulk carriers are also on hold. The vessels are expected to dock at Mundra and Kandla ports.

**Perspective & Context:**
- *In simple terms:* The war near Iran has made the Strait of Hormuz — a narrow 34-km sea passage through which much of the world's oil and gas flows — dangerous for ships. India depends heavily on LPG imports through this route, so the government is using high-level diplomacy to get its ships through safely. Two ships made it; several more are still waiting.
- **Strait of Hormuz (SoH)** — a narrow waterway between Iran and Oman connecting the Persian Gulf to the Arabian Sea. About 20% of the world's oil passes through it, making it the most critical energy chokepoint globally.
- **What India is doing:** Engaging at the highest diplomatic levels — PM and EAM — with all key stakeholders (Iran, GCC, US, Israel) to ensure uninterrupted energy supply. Some ships are being rerouted through Khor Fakkan Port on Sharjah's east coast, which operates outside the Strait.
- The ~2.76 lakh tonnes of LPG stuck at sea is nearly one full month of India's consumption — meaning any prolonged disruption could directly affect cooking gas availability for crores of Indian households.
- Globally, the US initiated a 400-million-barrel coordinated release from strategic petroleum reserves (including 86 million barrels from its own SPR) to stabilise oil prices amid the Hormuz disruption.

### SEBI Chief Says Markets Must Function Smoothly Even During Volatility

`High` | Capital Markets Regulation

SEBI Chairman Tuhin Kanta Pandey said financial markets must be designed to function efficiently during periods of uncertainty, noting that volatility is an inherent feature of capital markets. Speaking at the Moneycontrol Global Wealth Summit 2026 in Mumbai on March 15, Pandey pointed to the Middle East conflict's impact on energy supplies and global oil and gas market volatility, while drawing parallels with past disruptions during COVID-19 and the Russia-Ukraine war where markets eventually stabilised. He emphasised that the real strength of a capital market lies in the confidence that the system will function fairly, transparently, and efficiently even during stress. SEBI has undertaken initiatives including reducing the settlement cycle, expanding the Electronic Book Provider (EBP) mechanism to include REITs and InvITs, simplifying participation for global investors, and expanding investor participation.

**Perspective & Context:**
- *In simple terms:* India's top markets regulator is reassuring investors that the system is built to handle shocks like the current Middle East war. His message: don't panic-sell based on headlines; markets have survived similar crises before and recovered.
- **Electronic Book Provider (EBP)** — a platform for transparent price discovery in bond and debt markets, where issuers and investors can participate in an electronic bidding process. SEBI is now extending this to REITs and InvITs to bring the same transparency to infrastructure and real estate investment products.
- **REITs and InvITs** — Real Estate Investment Trusts and Infrastructure Investment Trusts allow retail investors to invest in income-generating real estate or infrastructure projects, similar to buying shares in a company but backed by physical assets like office buildings or toll roads.
- Pandey's advice to retail investors — "remain patient" — echoes a recurring regulatory theme: speed of information flow in modern markets means prices react to narratives faster than fundamentals, making knee-jerk reactions risky.

### Commerce Minister Goyal Promises More Support for Exporters Amid Gulf Conflict Disruptions

`High` | Trade & Commerce

Union Commerce Minister Piyush Goyal urged businesses and consumers not to panic amid trade disruptions caused by the Gulf conflict and announced that the government is preparing a concrete support agenda for exporters to be released the following week. Speaking at a business leaders awards event in Mumbai, Goyal said the Ministry has devised insurance cover schemes to protect export cargo that may be damaged, lost, or delayed due to Red Sea or Strait of Hormuz shipping disruptions. The government has set up 24-hour helplines for exporters and importers, established SOPs in coordination with export promotion councils, and is conducting inter-ministerial consultations. He acknowledged the war had created short-term challenges for trade, energy supplies, and financial markets. On the weakening rupee, Goyal pointed to the RBI's strong forex reserves — equivalent to nearly a year of imports — as a key buffer against currency pressure.

**Perspective & Context:**
- *In simple terms:* The Gulf war is disrupting shipping routes that Indian exporters depend on, causing delays, damaged cargo, and rising costs. The Commerce Minister is saying the government won't leave exporters to fend for themselves — insurance schemes, helplines, and a formal support package are coming.
- **What the government is doing:** Creating an insurance safety net so that if an exporter's cargo is damaged or delayed because ships can't pass through the Strait of Hormuz or Red Sea, the financial loss doesn't fall entirely on the exporter.
- **Export Promotion Councils** — industry bodies for specific sectors (textiles, engineering goods, spices, etc.) that work with the Commerce Ministry to promote and facilitate exports. They are now serving as the coordination layer between the government and affected exporters.
- India's forex reserves covering nearly a year of imports is a significant buffer — it means even if the rupee weakens due to global risk aversion, the RBI has substantial firepower to intervene and prevent a sharp depreciation.

### Gulf Food Prices Surge as Iran War Disrupts Logistics and Trade Routes

`Medium` | Global Economy

The Iran war has triggered food inflation in the Gulf region, particularly the UAE, with vegetable and fruit prices doubling and foodgrain stocks — especially rice — thinning since disruptions began on February 27. Tomato and onion prices in the UAE surged from 1 AED to 5–6 AED. The UAE reportedly has only a fortnight's stock of premium rice and may soon face potato shortages. Indian exports to the Gulf have been severely affected — over 150 tonnes of perishable exports from Kerala are stranded, with air freight costs from Kerala airports tripling from ₹70–80/kg to around ₹210/kg and container freight climbing to approximately $3,800. Traders are rerouting some cargo through Khor Fakkan Port on Sharjah's east coast, which operates outside the Strait of Hormuz. Some supplies are being managed through shipments from Sri Lanka, Vietnam, and Thailand, while Oman's road routes and ports are being used as alternatives since they bypass the Strait.

**Perspective & Context:**
- *In simple terms:* The war near Iran has choked off the main shipping routes to the Gulf, causing food prices to skyrocket in countries like the UAE that import most of their food. Indian exporters are particularly hit — fruits, vegetables, and coconuts from Kerala and Tamil Nadu can't get through affordably, and some shipments are simply being cancelled.
- **Why this matters for India:** The Gulf is a major market for Indian agricultural exports, especially perishables from southern states. Kerala alone has over 150 tonnes of goods stranded. The disruption also affects the large Indian diaspora in the Gulf who depend on these supplies, especially during Ramadan when demand peaks.
- **Khor Fakkan Port** — a port on the UAE's eastern coast (facing the Gulf of Oman), strategically important because it sits outside the Strait of Hormuz, allowing ships to bypass the chokepoint entirely. It is emerging as a critical alternative route during this crisis.
- Air freight tripling from ₹70–80 to ₹210/kg makes perishable exports commercially unviable — a ₹100 pineapple would need ₹210 just in shipping costs, pricing Indian produce out of the Gulf market.
