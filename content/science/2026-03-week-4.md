### PRAGYA: Bengaluru Startup Pranos Fusion Builds India's First Privately Developed Tokamak

Bengaluru-based startup Pranos Fusion has developed PRAGYA, India's first privately built tokamak — a compact nuclear fusion test device with a radius of just 40 cm, compared to the 6.2 m radius of the ITER (International Thermonuclear Experimental Reactor) under construction in France. PRAGYA was funded by a seed investment of $4,17,000 raised in May 2025 from angel investor Rahul Seth. The device is designed as a test bed for experiments in magnetohydrodynamic plasma stability, superconducting magnets, and auxiliary heating, enabling multiple experimental cycles without requiring large-scale facilities. A research paper on PRAGYA was co-authored by scientists from Pranos, the Jawaharlal Nehru Centre for Advanced Scientific Research (JNCASR, Bengaluru), and the Indian Institute of Science (IISc, Bengaluru). India has three other tokamaks, all government-operated under the Institute of Plasma Research (IPR) in Gandhinagar, Gujarat: ADITYA-1, ADITYA-U, and SST-1 (which uses superconducting magnets). Pranos is one of three Indian private fusion startups, alongside Hyderabad-based Hylenr and Bengaluru's Anubal Fusion, all of which have recently raised funding.

**Perspective & Context:**
- *In simple terms:* Nuclear fusion — the process that powers the sun — is considered the cleanest possible energy source. A tokamak is the doughnut-shaped device that tries to replicate this on Earth using powerful magnetic fields. India now has a tiny private version, built cheaply by a startup, designed not to generate power but to run experiments that could eventually help build one that does.
- **Tokamak** — a magnetic confinement device in which hot plasma (charged gas) is held in a ring-shaped chamber by magnetic fields. When hydrogen atoms fuse inside, they release enormous energy. The key challenge is sustaining the reaction efficiently enough to produce net energy output.
- **ITER** — the world's largest fusion project, a 35-nation collaboration in Cadarache, France (including India as a partner). PRAGYA at 40 cm radius is roughly 1/15th the scale of ITER (6.2 m radius), illustrating the contrast between a test bed and an industrial-scale experiment.
- India's three government tokamaks at IPR Gandhinagar: ADITYA-1 (oldest), ADITYA-U (upgraded), and SST-1 — the Steady-State Superconducting Tokamak, notable for its superconducting magnet system. All are significantly larger research machines than PRAGYA.
- At $4,17,000 (~₹3.5 crore), PRAGYA demonstrates that a functional test-bed tokamak can be built at a fraction of the cost of government research reactors — potentially democratising access to fusion research for academic institutions and startups.

### Turning Existing Telecom Fibre into a Distributed Sensor: Folium Sensing's Technology Explained

Bengaluru-based Folium Sensing is commercialising Distributed Fibre Optic Sensing (DFOS) technology, which converts existing telecom-grade optical fibre cables into continuous real-time sensors capable of monitoring over tens of kilometres simultaneously. The technology exploits three types of light scattering within the fibre: Rayleigh scattering (detects vibrations and acoustic events — used for pipeline leak detection and intrusion monitoring); Raman scattering (sensitive to temperature variations — applied in fire detection and power cable monitoring); and Brillouin scattering (measures mechanical stress and deformation — used for structural health monitoring of bridges and tunnels). AI/ML models filter critical events from background noise. IIT-Madras has built a 100-metre buried pipeline test bed to simulate threats including leaks, manual digging, and earthmovers. A single system monitors up to 100 km of fibre; the world record for distributed sensing exceeds 2,200 km. Applications span oil and gas pipelines, border surveillance, railway track monitoring, power cable health monitoring, and aerospace (monitoring combustion instability in jet engines).

**Perspective & Context:**
- *In simple terms:* Every underground telecom cable is already picking up vibrations, temperature changes, and physical signals from the world around it. Folium Sensing has built a device that listens to these signals — using the same fibre that carries internet traffic — and turns the entire cable into a distributed sensor that can detect a pipeline leak, a border intruder, or an animal crossing a railway track, all from a central control room.
- **Distributed sensing vs point sensors** — a thermometer measures conditions at one fixed location. Distributed fibre sensing continuously monitors conditions at every metre along the fibre's entire length simultaneously — like having thousands of thermometers, microphones, and strain gauges all wired up, with hardware only at the two ends.
- **Backscattering** — when a laser pulse travels through optical fibre, a tiny fraction of light scatters backwards. The characteristics of this returning light encode physical conditions (temperature, vibration, strain) at every point along the cable.
- **Strategic implications for India:** Border areas already have underground fibre for surveillance cameras. Folium's system can fill "blind spots" between cameras by turning the connecting fibre itself into a motion sensor. Oil and gas pipeline monitoring can also replace workers who currently patrol 50 km stretches on motorbikes daily — a safety, cost, and reliability improvement.
- Power is only needed at the central control room — not along the cable — making the system well-suited for remote or border locations where power supply is unreliable or unavailable.

### Why Electric Vehicle Batteries Catch Fire — Thermal Runaway Explained

Nearly all electric vehicles use lithium-ion batteries, which pack thousands of cells tightly together. The primary fire risk is thermal runaway — a chain reaction where one overheating cell causes neighbouring cells to overheat faster than the cooling system can cope, releasing toxic and flammable gases including hydrogen fluoride. Common triggers include hard impacts that puncture or deform cells, overcharging through uncertified or damaged chargers, rare manufacturing defects that cause internal short circuits, and sustained high temperatures from hot weather or charging immediately after long drives. Flooding can also infiltrate battery packs and cause short circuits. While petrol cars catch fire more frequently, EV battery fires burn hotter, spread faster, and are harder to extinguish because the battery releases oxygen as it burns. On the safety front, manufacturers are developing phase-change cooling systems (where coolant evaporates to absorb heat more efficiently), solid-state electrolytes to replace flammable liquid electrolytes, and improved internal firewalls between cells. The Bureau of Indian Standards released updated EV battery safety norms in 2023, and the Automotive Research Association of India's AIS-156 standard mandates thermal propagation tests and requires battery packs to give occupants at least five minutes' warning before a fire.

**Perspective & Context:**
- *In simple terms:* An EV battery is like thousands of tiny rechargeable cells packed together. If one cell overheats and its neighbours can't be cooled fast enough, you get a domino effect — thermal runaway — that can lead to a fire. The article was prompted by a fatal house fire in Indore on March 18 linked to an EV charging point, which killed eight people.
- **Thermal runaway** — a self-accelerating chain reaction where heat from one failing battery cell triggers neighbouring cells to fail too, releasing flammable gases. Think of it like one match lighting the next in a tightly packed row, except each "match" also releases toxic fumes.
- **Battery Management System (BMS)** — an onboard computer that monitors each cell's temperature, voltage, and current, keeping the battery within safe operating limits. It's the battery's "immune system" — when it works, problems are caught early; when it's bypassed (e.g., by uncertified chargers), risks multiply.
- **Solid-state batteries** — next-generation batteries that replace the liquid electrolyte (which can leak and catch fire) with a solid material. They promise to be safer, lighter, and more energy-dense, but are still years from mass production.
- A lithium-ion battery fire can reach 600–1,000°C — hot enough to melt aluminium. Firefighters often need 10–40 times more water to extinguish an EV fire compared to a petrol car fire, and batteries can reignite hours or even days later.

### CERN Scientists Successfully Transport Antiprotons by Road for the First Time

Scientists at the European Organization for Nuclear Research (CERN) in Geneva successfully transported approximately 100 antiprotons by truck in a first-of-its-kind road test on Tuesday. The antiprotons — antimatter counterparts of protons — were suspended in a vacuum inside a specially designed 1,000 kg transportable antiproton trap, held in place by superconducting magnets cooled to -269°C. The half-hour drive tested whether the particles could survive road transport without coming into contact with matter, which would instantly annihilate them. Roughly 91 of 100 antiprotons survived the full journey. The experiment, led by Stefan Ulmer, is a first step toward eventually delivering CERN-produced antiprotons to researchers at Heinrich Heine University in Düsseldorf, Germany — an eight-hour drive away. A key limitation remains: the trap can sustain antiprotons on its own for only about four hours, half the time needed for the Düsseldorf trip.

**Perspective & Context:**
- *In simple terms:* CERN scientists put some of the rarest particles in the universe — antiprotons — in a specially designed box and drove them around in a truck. The challenge: if even one antiproton touches ordinary matter, it's instantly destroyed in a flash of energy. 91 out of 100 survived, making this a successful proof of concept for moving antimatter outside the lab.
- **Antimatter** — for every particle of ordinary matter, there exists a mirror-image "antiparticle" with the opposite charge. When matter meets antimatter, both are annihilated, releasing energy (as described by Einstein's E=mc²). Antimatter is extraordinarily rare in nature and expensive to produce — a gram of antihydrogen would theoretically cost ~$62.5 trillion.
- **Why transport antimatter at all?** CERN is one of the only places that can produce antiprotons. If scientists elsewhere could receive them, it would dramatically expand antimatter research — particularly high-precision comparisons between matter and antimatter that could reveal why the universe is made almost entirely of matter, one of physics' biggest unsolved puzzles.
- The trap's -269°C magnets are just 4 degrees above absolute zero — colder than outer space (~-270.5°C). At these temperatures, superconducting magnets can generate powerful magnetic fields with zero electrical resistance, keeping antiprotons levitated away from the trap's matter walls.
- The total mass transported was less than 100 hydrogen atoms — so tiny that even if all antiprotons annihilated at once, the energy release would be undetectable without an oscilloscope.
